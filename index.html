About Me

A curiosity that never landed

Aviation, for me, started as a question I couldn't put down: how does something so heavy stay in the air, and who makes sure it keeps doing so? That question has quietly shaped most of what I choose to spend my time on.

I'm drawn to the systems behind flight, especially the avionics and electronics that let a pilot trust their instruments. I enjoy taking a technical problem apart, understanding why it behaves the way it does, and working patiently toward a fix. A lot of what I know I've picked up outside the classroom, through competitions, an online flight-mechanics course, and simply reading about how aircraft work.

Leadership has been the other half of my growth. Guiding junior cadets and leading a competition team taught me that engineering rarely happens alone. It depends on communication, trust, and doing the unglamorous work well.

My long-term goal is to become an RSAF pilot, and a strong foundation in aerospace electronics is how I intend to get there, by understanding the aircraft from the inside out before I ever fly one.

Why Aerospace Electronics

The right foundation for where I'm going

I chose Aerospace Electronics because it provides the technical knowledge needed to understand modern aircraft systems, and because it sits exactly where my engineering curiosity and my aviation ambitions meet.

Flight theory that supports my goal

The Commercial Flight Theory elective strengthens my understanding of flight, complementing my long-term aspiration of becoming an RSAF pilot.

Hands-on engineering skills

The avionics, electronics and aircraft-wiring modules let me build practical skills rather than just theory, which is the kind of work I already enjoy.

Real industry exposure

Internships and partnerships with aerospace companies would place me inside real aerospace environments and prepare me for future opportunities.

A foundation for both paths

Whether in engineering or the cockpit, this diploma gives me the ideal technical grounding, plus a connection to the Singapore Youth Flying Club.

My Journey

From a flypast to a flight path

The moments, in and out of uniform, that turned a childhood question into a direction.

2018

The beginning

At the Marina Bay National Day Parade, I watched RSAF F-15SGs and F-16Cs roar overhead with my family. I wanted to know how aircraft stay up, how they manoeuvre, and how their electronic systems work together. That curiosity became my starting point.

2023

Joined the NPCC

I joined the National Police Cadet Corps, where discipline, teamwork and resilience stopped being words and became daily habits. It's where I first learned to lead by doing.

images/journey-2023-npcc.jpg
2023 – Present

Class Chairperson

Leading my class taught me organisation and communication under real pressure. I delegated tasks, coordinated classroom projects, and kept a group of peers moving in the same direction.

images/journey-2023-chairperson.jpg
2025

Cadet Leader, Staff Sergeant

As a Staff Sergeant I trained and guided junior cadets through their training. Watching them grow into capable, confident cadets is the part of leadership I'm proudest of.

2025 – 2026

PREPMUN & DHAPMUN

Model United Nations pushed me well outside my comfort zone. Preparing positions and debating them sharpened my public speaking, research, critical thinking and confidence.

images/journey-2026-mun.jpg
2026

Basic Flight Mechanics

I completed an online Basic Flight Mechanics course, working through lift, drag, thrust, control surfaces and how an aircraft turns. It gave me the vocabulary to understand what I'd only watched from the ground.

images/journey-2026-coursera.jpg
2026

Singapore Amazing Flying Machine Competition

As team leader, I ran our planning, design, building, testing, photography and presentation. We worked through a nose-heavy airframe, wings that kept breaking, missing teammates, and an aircraft that spun 720° on a test flight. Solving each problem one at a time taught me real resilience, and we came away with a participation certificate and a lot more than that.

images/journey-2026-safmc.jpg
2026

Singapore Airshow

Seeing fifth-generation fighters in person for the first time, the F-35A and F-35B, was unforgettable, along with the F-16 and the AIM-9 Sidewinder and AIM-120 AMRAAM. Looking into real cockpits and avionics made me realise how much there still is to learn, and pulled me further toward aerospace electronics.

images/journey-2026-airshow.jpg
2026

Singapore Polytechnic Open House

Visiting the AeroHub and the commercial flight simulator showed me exactly what studying aerospace electronics would feel like. The Commercial Flight Theory elective, avionics and aircraft-wiring modules, internships with aerospace companies, and the link to the Singapore Youth Flying Club made the choice clear, and gave me a strong foundation for my goal of becoming an RSAF pilot.

images/journey-2026-sp.jpg
2026

GE Aerospace Electrical Engineering Simulation

This is where aerospace electronics stopped being theory for me. In GE Aerospace's Explore Electrical Engineering job simulation I designed an aircraft electrical system from scratch, planned its power distribution and picked the right circuit breakers to real aerospace standards, then traced a navigation fault back to its cause under harsh conditions. Diagnosing avionics problems, running simulations to see how the environment affects reliability, and writing proper design proposals to strict safety protocols made this the closest I have come to the work I genuinely want to do.
